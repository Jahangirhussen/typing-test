Typing Lessons

Structured guides for learning touch typing from scratch — English fundamentals, Bangla input methods, keyboard layout, and shortcuts used on this site.

English Typing Lessons

Touch typing means typing without looking at the keyboard, using consistent finger assignments for every key. It feels slower at first, but it's the only technique that scales to high speed with low error rates. Follow this progression:

  1. Learn the home row first. Rest your left hand on A S D F and your right hand on J K L ;. Your fingers should return to these eight keys after every keystroke. Most keyboards have a small raised bump on F and J so you can find the home row by feel.
  2. Fix your posture before your speed. Sit with your back straight, elbows roughly at a 90-degree angle, wrists floating (not resting on the desk while typing), and the screen at eye level. Poor posture causes fatigue long before it causes typos, and fatigue is what actually slows you down over a long session.
  3. Add one row at a time. Once the home row feels automatic, extend to the top row (Q W E R T Y U I O P) and then the bottom row (Z X C V B N M), always returning each finger to its home key between presses.
  4. Build speed gradually, not immediately. Use the Beginner and Easy difficulty practice on this site until your accuracy is consistently above 95%, then move up to Medium, Hard, and Expert. Chasing WPM before accuracy just trains you to type fast and wrong, which is a harder habit to unlearn later.

A realistic goal for a beginner is 20-30 WPM after a few weeks of daily practice, 40-60 WPM is a solid intermediate level, and touch typists who practice regularly often reach 70-100+ WPM.

Bangla Typing Lessons

Bangla typing on modern computers is almost always done through an Input Method Editor (IME) that converts your keystrokes into Bangla Unicode characters, since standard keyboards are printed with Latin letters. There are two dominant approaches used across Bangladesh and West Bengal:

  • Avro (phonetic): you type the Bangla word the way it sounds using English letters, and the IME converts it into Bangla script as you type. This is the easier method to learn because it needs no memorization — see the Avro Guide below.
  • Bijoy (traditional/fixed-key): each key on the keyboard is mapped directly to a specific Bangla character or symbol, similar to how QWERTY maps to English letters. It requires memorizing the layout but is the long-standing standard in Bangladeshi print and publishing — see the Bijoy Guide below.

On this site, the practice text itself is always displayed as proper Bangla Unicode — your job is simply to reproduce it using whichever input method you already have installed on your operating system. Beginners are usually best served by starting with Avro since it lets you focus on accuracy without first memorizing a new key layout.

Finger Placement

Each finger is responsible for a fixed set of keys, radiating out from the home row. Here is the standard assignment (rows shown top to bottom: number row omitted for simplicity):

Left hand                          Right hand
Pinky  Ring  Middle Index          Index Middle Ring  Pinky
  Q     W     E     R    T    Y    U     I     O     P
  A     S     D     F    G    H    J     K     L     ;
  Z     X     C     V    B    N    M     ,     .     /
                    [both thumbs] -> SPACE

F and J are the anchor keys — find their raised bumps by feel and rest your index fingers there. From that position: left pinky covers Q A Z and Shift/Tab area, left ring covers W S X, left middle covers E D C, left index covers R F V plus reaches to T G B. Mirror this on the right: right index covers U J M plus reaches to Y H N, right middle covers I K ,, right ring covers O L ., right pinky covers P ; / and Enter/Shift. Both thumbs share the spacebar.

Keyboard Guide

Most keyboards sold worldwide use the QWERTY layout, named after the first six letters on the top-left letter row. QWERTY was designed in the 1870s for mechanical typewriters and has remained the default ever since, despite alternative layouts like Dvorak and Colemak claiming ergonomic advantages — QWERTY's advantage is simply that almost everyone already knows it.

A keyboard is broadly divided into zones: the function row (F1-F12) along the top for shortcuts and system controls; the number row below it with digits and symbols; the main alphanumeric block containing the QWERTY letters, Tab, Caps Lock, Shift, and Enter; a navigation cluster (arrow keys, Home, End, Page Up/Down) usually to the right; and an optional numeric keypad on full-size keyboards for fast number entry with one hand. Laptop keyboards often compress or omit the keypad and navigation cluster to save space.

Two keys worth learning early: Caps Lock toggles all-uppercase typing and is best avoided for normal text (use Shift for occasional capitals instead), and Tab is used on this site specifically to restart the current typing test — see the shortcuts table below.

Avro Guide

Avro Phonetic is a free Bangla typing method (and software, originally by OmicronLab) where you type a Bangla word phonetically using English letters, and it is automatically transliterated into Bangla Unicode script. It's the most beginner-friendly way to type Bangla because there is no new key layout to memorize — you just spell out the sound of the word.

Because it's rule-based on phonetics rather than fixed keys, Avro is widely recommended for anyone who already types comfortably in English and wants to add Bangla without learning a second layout from scratch.

Bijoy Guide

Bijoy (also written Bijoy Bayanno / বিজয়) is the older, fixed-key Bangla keyboard layout that has been the de facto standard in Bangladeshi newspapers, publishing houses, and government offices since the early 1990s. Unlike Avro's phonetic approach, Bijoy maps each physical key directly to a specific Bangla character — pressing a given key always produces the same Bangla letter or symbol, regardless of how the word sounds in English.

This means Bijoy typists memorize the position of every consonant, vowel sign, and conjunct the same way an English typist memorizes QWERTY — there is no transliteration step, and typing speed can end up very high once the layout is internalized, since experienced professional typesetters have used it for decades. The trade-off is a steeper learning curve for newcomers: you cannot "guess" a key position from the sound of a word the way you can with Avro.

Historically, Bijoy predates most Unicode-based Bangla input tools and was originally built around a proprietary (non-Unicode) font encoding, which is why older Bijoy-typed documents sometimes display incorrectly unless converted to Unicode. Modern Bijoy-compatible input methods can now output standard Bangla Unicode, making the text compatible with the rest of this site and the modern web.

Unicode Guide

Unicode is a single, universal character-encoding standard that assigns a unique number to every letter, digit, and symbol across virtually all the world's writing systems, so text displays consistently across different devices, operating systems, and apps. Bangla script has its own dedicated Unicode block (the "Bengali" block, code points U+0980 to U+09FF) covering all Bangla consonants, vowels, vowel signs, and conjunct-forming characters.

Before Unicode became universal, Bangla text online relied on custom, non-standard fonts (including the original Bijoy encoding), which meant a document typed on one computer could turn into unreadable boxes or garbled Latin characters on another unless the exact same font was installed. Because every Bangla text on this site is stored and rendered as proper Unicode, it displays correctly on any modern browser or device, copies and pastes cleanly into other Unicode-aware software, and is searchable and accessible the same way English text is.

Lessons FAQ

How long does it take to learn touch typing?

Most people build reasonable home-row comfort within one to two weeks of short daily practice, and reach a fluent 40+ WPM within one to three months, depending on how consistently they practice.

Should I learn Avro or Bijoy first?

If you already type comfortably in English, start with Avro — it's phonetic, so there's no new layout to memorize. Learn Bijoy later if you need compatibility with traditional Bangladeshi publishing workflows.

Do I need to install anything to type Bangla here?

Yes, an operating-system-level Bangla input method (Avro Keyboard, Bijoy, or your OS's built-in Bangla IME) is required to type Bangla characters. This site only displays the Bangla Unicode text you need to reproduce.

Why do my fingers hurt after practicing?

This is almost always a posture or tension problem, not a typing-technique problem. Keep wrists floating rather than resting on the desk, relax your shoulders, and take short breaks every 15-20 minutes.

What's a realistic WPM goal for a beginner?

Aim for 20-30 WPM with high accuracy in your first few weeks. Speed should always follow accuracy — a fast but error-filled test is not actually faster once you account for corrections.
