8 Tips to Type Faster

1. Improve Accuracy Before Speed

Accuracy is the ceiling on your real-world speed — every mistyped character costs you the time to notice it, correct it, and re-type it. Practice at a pace where you can stay above 95% accuracy first, then let your speed rise naturally as the correct motions become automatic. Chasing raw WPM early just trains bad habits into muscle memory.

2. Learn Touch Typing

Touch typing — typing without looking at the keyboard — removes the constant eye movement between screen and keys that silently drains your speed. It feels awkward for the first week or two, but every touch typist eventually types faster than every "hunt and peck" typist, no exceptions. See the Finger Placement guide to get started.

3. Use All Ten Fingers

Many self-taught typists develop reasonable speed using only four to six fingers, which caps out lower than a full ten-finger technique. Each finger should own a specific set of keys (see the finger-placement chart in the Lessons section) so no single finger is overworked while others sit idle.

4. Maintain Good Posture

Sit with your back straight, feet flat on the floor, elbows near a 90-degree angle, and wrists floating just above the keyboard rather than resting on the desk. Poor posture causes fatigue and tension that slow you down gradually over a session, even if it doesn't feel like a problem in the first five minutes.

5. Practice Daily in Short Sessions

Ten to fifteen focused minutes a day builds muscle memory faster than one long, tiring session per week. Consistency compounds — short daily reps keep the correct finger motions fresh, while infrequent long sessions let bad habits creep back in between practices.

6. Learn Common Bigrams and Digraphs

Certain letter pairs — "th," "he," "in," "er," "an," "re" — appear constantly in English, and typing them as a fluid two-key motion rather than two separate keystrokes measurably increases speed. Notice which pairs you type slowly and give them extra deliberate reps.

7. Take Regular Breaks

Typing is repetitive strain on a small set of muscles and tendons. A short break every 15-20 minutes — standing, stretching your fingers and wrists — prevents the fatigue that quietly increases your error rate in longer sessions and protects you from repetitive strain injuries over the long run.

8. Track Your Progress Over Time

Numbers you can't see are easy to plateau on. Use the stats and history tracking built into this site to watch your WPM, CPM, and accuracy trend across sessions, and notice which difficulty levels or content types are your weak points so you can target practice there.

Tips FAQ

Should I focus on speed or accuracy first?

Always accuracy first. A typo costs more time to fix than it would have taken to type the character correctly the first time, so a slower-but-accurate typist often finishes faster in practice than a fast-but-sloppy one.

How often should I practice to see improvement?

Short daily sessions of 10-15 minutes tend to outperform occasional long sessions, because consistency reinforces the correct finger motions before they fade.

Is it too late to switch from hunt-and-peck to touch typing?

No — it's a common transition. Expect a temporary dip in speed for one to two weeks as you rebuild the habit, followed by significantly higher long-term speed than hunt-and-peck ever allows.

Do these tips apply to Bangla typing too?

Yes. Accuracy-first practice, good posture, ten-finger technique, and daily short sessions all apply equally whether you're typing English on QWERTY or Bangla through Avro or Bijoy.
